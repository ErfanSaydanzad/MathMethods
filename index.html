Preface

About These Notes

These lecture notes were prepared for PHYS 3260: Mathematical Physics at Kennesaw State University. Their purpose is practical: to help students develop the mathematical tools that appear again and again in mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum mechanics, waves, and thermal physics. The emphasis is not only on carrying out calculations, but also on recognizing why a particular method is useful and what its result means physically.

The notes begin with infinite series, convergence tests, power series, and Taylor expansions, since these ideas underlie approximation throughout physics. Complex numbers are then introduced as a natural language for oscillations, waves, exponentials, and many later topics. The chapter on linear algebra develops systems of equations, matrices, determinants, vector spaces, orthogonality, eigenvalues, diagonalization, and applications such as normal modes of a linear molecule. These topics provide the algebraic foundation for much of modern physics, including quantum mechanics.

The middle part of the course focuses on calculus of several variables. Partial differentiation, total differentials, chain rules, coordinate transformations, multiple integrals, and Jacobians are presented with applications to thermodynamics, geometry, and physical coordinate systems. Vector analysis then extends these ideas to scalar and vector fields, gradients, divergences, curls, line integrals, surface integrals, volume integrals, conservative fields, Green's theorem, Stokes's theorem, the divergence theorem, and Maxwell's equations.

The final part of the notes develops methods for representing and solving physical systems. Fourier series and Fourier transforms are used to decompose functions into modes. Ordinary differential equations are treated from first-order equations through second-order linear equations, including the forced and damped harmonic oscillator. Series solutions lead to Legendre polynomials, associated Legendre functions, spherical harmonics, Bessel functions, Hankel functions, and orthogonal eigenfunction bases. The last chapter applies these tools to partial differential equations, separation of variables, Laplace's equation, the Helmholtz equation, and the infinite spherical well.

These notes are intended to be read actively. Work through the examples with pencil and paper, check each algebraic step, and ask what physical quantity each symbol represents. Some derivations are written in detail so that the method can be followed without hiding essential steps; other passages are meant to summarize a pattern that will become clearer through homework and repeated use.

The organization is influenced by standard mathematical physics references, especially Mathematical Methods in the Physical Sciences by Mary L. Boas, but the presentation is adapted to this course and to the needs of our students. The goal is to balance technique with intuition, rigor with accessibility, and mathematical structure with physical interpretation.

I hope these notes serve as both a guide during the course and a useful reference afterward. Mathematical methods become powerful when they are practiced patiently and connected to real physical problems; this text is meant to support that process.
