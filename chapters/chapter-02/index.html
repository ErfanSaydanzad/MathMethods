Chapter 2

Complex Numbers

Introduction to Complex Numbers

Motivation

In algebra, the quadratic formula

\[ z = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \]

provides the general solution of a quadratic equation

\[ a z^2 + b z + c = 0. \]

When the discriminant

\[ d = b^2 - 4ac \]

is negative, the square root involves a negative number. To extend the number system so that such roots exist, we introduce a new quantity:

\[ i = \sqrt{-1}, \quad i^2 = -1. \]
Definition - Complex Numbers

A complex number is any number of the form

\[ z = x + i y, \]

where \(x, y \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(i\) is the imaginary unit defined by \(i^2 = -1\).

  • \(x\) is the real part of \(z\): \(\Re(z) = x\).

  • \(y\) is the imaginary part of \(z\): \(\Im(z) = y\).

Both \(x\) and \(y\) may be positive, negative, or zero.

Special Cases

  • If \(y = 0\), then \(z\) is purely real: \(z = x\).

  • If \(x = 0\) and \(y \neq 0\), then \(z\) is purely imaginary: \(z = i y\).

Example

  1. \(z = 4 + 3i\): \(\Re(z) = 4\), \(\Im(z) = 3\).

  2. \(z = -5i\): \(\Re(z) = 0\), \(\Im(z) = -5\) (pure imaginary).

  3. \(z = 7\): \(\Re(z) = 7\), \(\Im(z) = 0\) (pure real).

  4. \(z = -2 + i\sqrt{5}\): \(\Re(z) = -2\), \(\Im(z) = \sqrt{5}\).

Example

Express \(\sqrt{-50}\) in the form \(x + iy\).

Solution.

\[ \sqrt{-50} = \sqrt{-1 \cdot 25 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{-1} \cdot \sqrt{25} \cdot \sqrt{2} = i \cdot 5 \sqrt{2} = 5\sqrt{2}\, i. \]

Motivation from Algebra

Consider

\[ z^2 - 2z + 2 = 0. \]

Applying the quadratic formula:

\[ z = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 8}}{2} = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2} = \frac{2 \pm 2i}{2} = 1 \pm i. \]

The solutions \(1 \pm i\) are complex numbers with both real and imaginary parts nonzero.

Example

Solve \(z^2 + z + 1 = 0\).

Solution.

\[ z = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{-3}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm i\sqrt{3}}{2}. \]

Thus, the solutions are

\[ z = -\frac12 + i\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad z = -\frac12 - i\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}. \]

The Complex Plane

Definition - Geometric Interpretation of Complex Numbers

A complex number \(z = x + iy\) can be represented as a point \((x, y)\) in a two-dimensional coordinate plane, called the complex plane or Argand diagram.

  • The horizontal axis represents the real part \(x = \Re(z)\).

  • The vertical axis represents the imaginary part \(y = \Im(z)\).

Remark - Rectangular Form

When we write \(z = x + iy\), we say \(z\) is in rectangular form, because \((x, y)\) are the rectangular coordinates of the point representing \(z\).

Remark - Polar Form

We can also locate a point using polar coordinates \((r, \theta)\):

\[ x = r \cos \theta, \quad y = r \sin \theta. \]

Then

\[ z = x + iy = r (\cos\theta + i \sin\theta). \]

Later we will see that \(\cos\theta + i \sin\theta = e^{i\theta}\), giving the compact form

\[ z = r e^{i\theta}. \]

Diagram of the Complex Plane

Radians vs Degrees

In calculus and advanced mathematics, radians are the standard measure for angles.

  • Many formulas, such as \(\frac{d}{dx} \sin x = \cos x\), are valid only if \(x\) is in radians.

  • For computation, degrees can be used if the context allows, but be careful when substituting into formulas derived with radians.

Worked Examples

Example

Represent \(1 + i\sqrt{3}\) in polar form.

Solution.

\[ r = \sqrt{1^2 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{1 + 3} = 2, \]
\[ \theta = \arctan\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{1}\right) = \frac{\pi}{3}. \]

Thus:

\[ 1 + i\sqrt{3} = 2 \left(\cos \frac{\pi}{3} + i \sin \frac{\pi}{3} \right) = 2 e^{i\pi/3}. \]
Example

Convert \(3 e^{-i\pi/2}\) to rectangular form.

Solution.

\[ \cos\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 0, \quad \sin\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = -1. \]

So:

\[ 3 e^{-i\pi/2} = 3\left(0 - i\right) = -3i. \]

Terminology and Notation

Definition - Standard Notation for Complex Numbers

For a complex number

\[ z = x + iy = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = r e^{i\theta}, \]

we write:

\[ \Re(z) = x, \qquad \Im(z) = y, \qquad |z| = r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \qquad \arg(z) = \theta. \]

The principal argument, denoted \(\arg(z)\), is the unique angle

\[ \Theta \in (-\pi, \pi] \quad \text{such that} \quad z = |z| e^{i\Theta}. \]
Remark

Always identify the correct quadrant when finding \(\theta=\arg(z)\); formulas like \(\theta=\arctan(y/x)\) must be adjusted to land in the correct quadrant.

Complex Conjugate

Definition - Complex Conjugate

The complex conjugate of a complex number \(z = x + iy\) is defined by

\[ \overline{z} = x - iy. \]

Geometrically, \(\overline{z}\) is the reflection of the point \(z\) across the real axis in the complex plane.

Basic Identities

For all \(z,w\in\mathbb{C}\):

\[ z\overline{z}=|z|^2,\qquad \overline{z+w}=\overline{z}+\overline{w},\qquad \overline{zw}=\overline{z}\,\overline{w},\qquad \overline{\left(\frac{z}{w}\right)}=\frac{\overline{z}}{\overline{w}}\ (w\neq 0). \]

Useful reconstructions:

\[ \Re(z)=\frac{z+\overline{z}}{2},\qquad \Im(z)=\frac{z-\overline{z}}{2i}. \]

Worked Examples

Example - Polar data from rectangular

Write \(z=-1-i\) in polar form.

Solution.

\[ |z|=\sqrt{(-1)^2+(-1)^2}=\sqrt{2},\qquad \theta=\arg(z)=-\frac{3\pi}{4}\ \ (\text{III quadrant}). \]

Hence

\[ z=\sqrt{2}\,e^{-3\pi i/4}=\sqrt{2}\big(\cos(-3\pi/4)+i\sin(-3\pi/4)\big). \]
Example - Extracting real/imag via conjugates

Let \( z=\dfrac{2-3i}{1+i} \). Compute \( |z| \), \( \Re(z) \), and \( \Im(z) \).

Solution.

\[ z=\frac{(2-3i)(1-i)}{(1+i)(1-i)}=\frac{2-2i-3i+3i^2}{1- i^2} =\frac{2-5i-3}{2}=\frac{-1-5i}{2}. \]

Thus \( \Re(z)=-\tfrac{1}{2},\ \Im(z)=-\tfrac{5}{2} \), and

\[ |z|=\sqrt{\Big(-\frac{1}{2}\Big)^2+\Big(-\frac{5}{2}\Big)^2} =\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{1+25}=\frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}. \]

(Alternatively, \( |z|=\sqrt{z\overline{z}} \).)

Quick Facts

  • Multiplication/division in polar form:

    \[ (r_1e^{i\theta_1})(r_2e^{i\theta_2})=(r_1r_2)e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)},\quad \frac{r_1e^{i\theta_1}}{r_2e^{i\theta_2}}=\left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)e^{i(\theta_1-\theta_2)}. \]

  • Conjugation in polar form: \( \overline{re^{i\theta}}=re^{-i\theta} \).

  • Equality: \( z_1=z_2 \iff \Re(z_1)=\Re(z_2)\ \text{and}\ \Im(z_1)=\Im(z_2) \).

Complex Algebra

In this section, we develop the algebraic techniques needed to manipulate complex numbers in both rectangular form \(x + iy\) and polar form \(re^{i\theta}\). The key operations include:

  • addition and subtraction,

  • multiplication and division,

  • complex conjugation and absolute value,

  • converting between \(x+iy\) and \(re^{i\theta}\),

  • solving equations involving complex numbers,

  • interpreting algebraic relationships geometrically in the complex plane.

These tools allow us to treat complex numbers as a natural extension of real numbers while benefiting from their geometric interpretation.

Simplifying to \(x+iy\) or \(re^{i\theta}\)

For \(z_1=x_1+iy_1\) and \(z_2=x_2+iy_2\):

\[ z_1\pm z_2=(x_1\pm x_2)+i(y_1\pm y_2),\qquad z_1z_2=(x_1x_2-y_1y_2)+i(x_1y_2+x_2y_1). \]

To divide, multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator:

\[ \frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{z_1\overline{z_2}}{|z_2|^2},\quad z_2\neq 0. \]
Example - Power and product
\[ (1+i)^2 = 1+2i+i^2 = 2i,\qquad (2+i)(3-i) = 6-2i+3i - i^2 = 7+i. \]
Example - Division to \(x+iy\)

\( \dfrac{2+i}{3-i} = \dfrac{(2+i)(3+i)}{(3-i)(3+i)} = \dfrac{6+5i+i^2}{3^2-(-1)} = \dfrac{5+5i}{10} = \frac12+\frac12 i. \)

Conjugates of Expressions

The conjugate distributes over sums/products/quotients:

\[ \overline{z_1\pm z_2}=\overline{z_1}\pm \overline{z_2},\quad \overline{z_1 z_2}=\overline{z_1}\,\overline{z_2},\quad \overline{\Big(\frac{z_1}{z_2}\Big)}=\frac{\overline{z_1}}{\overline{z_2}}\ (z_2\neq 0). \]

Absolute Value (Modulus)

\[ |z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=\sqrt{z\,\overline{z}},\qquad |zw|=|z|\,|w|,\qquad \Big|\frac{z}{w}\Big|=\frac{|z|}{|w|}. \]
Example

\( \displaystyle \left|\frac{\sqrt{5}+3i}{\,1-i\,}\right| =\frac{\sqrt{(\sqrt{5})^2+3^2}}{\sqrt{1^2+(-1)^2}} =\frac{\sqrt{14}}{\sqrt{2}}=\sqrt{7}. \)

Complex Equations

Two complex numbers are equal iff their real parts are equal and their imaginary parts are equal. Often, write \(z=x+iy\) and split into real/imaginary equations.

Example

Solve \((x+iy)^2=2i\) for real \(x,y\).

Solution.

\[ (x+iy)^2=(x^2-y^2)+i(2xy)=0+2i \]

Equate real and imaginary parts:

\[ \begin{cases} x^2-y^2=0,\\[2pt] 2xy=2. \end{cases} \]

From \(x^2-y^2=(x-y)(x+y)=0\) we have \(y=x\) or \(y=-x\).

Case 1: \(y=x\). Then \(2xy=2\Rightarrow 2x^2=2\Rightarrow x^2=1\). Hence \(x=\pm 1\) and \(y=x\). This gives \(1+i\) and \(-1-i\).

Case 2: \(y=-x\). Then \(2xy=2\Rightarrow 2x(-x)=2\Rightarrow -2x^2=2\Rightarrow x^2=-1\), which is impossible for real \(x\). So no solutions from this branch.

Therefore the solutions are

\[ (x,y)=(1,1)\quad\text{and}\quad(x,y)=(-1,-1), \]

i.e. \(z=1+i\) and \(z=-1-i\).

Check. \((1+i)^2=1+2i-1=2i\) and \((-1-i)^2=1+2i-1=2i\).

(Alternative via polar form.) \(2i=2e^{i\pi/2}\). Square roots have modulus \(\sqrt{2}\) and arguments \(\frac{\pi}{4}\) and \(\frac{\pi}{4}+\pi\), giving \(\sqrt{2}e^{i\pi/4}=1+i\) and \(\sqrt{2}e^{i5\pi/4}=-1-i\).

Graphs (Geometry in the Complex Plane)

Identify loci via \(z=x+iy\), \(|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\), and distances:

\[ |z-a|=R \ \text{is a circle of radius }R\text{ centered at }a\in\mathbb{C}. \]
\[ \Re z = c \ \text{is the vertical line } x=c;\quad \Im z = c \ \text{is the horizontal line } y=c. \]
Example

\( |z-1|=2 \) is the circle \((x-1)^2+y^2=4\). \( \angle z = \pi/4 \) is the ray \(y=x\) with \(x>0\).

Complex Infinite Series

Definition - Complex Series

A complex series is an expression of the form

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} z_n, \qquad z_n \in \mathbb{C}. \]

Let

\[ S_N = \sum_{n=0}^{N} z_n \]

be the sequence of partial sums. If the limit

\[ \lim_{N \to \infty} S_N = S \]

exists and is a finite complex number \(S\), then the series converges, and we write

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} z_n = S. \]

If the limit does not exist or is not finite, the series diverges.

Theorem - Convergence of Complex Series

Let \(z_n = a_n + i b_n\) with \(a_n, b_n \in \mathbb{R}\). Then

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty z_n \ \text{converges} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n \ \text{and} \ \sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n \ \text{both converge}. \]

In that case, the sum satisfies

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty z_n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n + i \sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n. \]
Remark

In practice, check convergence of the real and imaginary parts separately, or use absolute convergence to simplify the test.

Theorem - Absolute Convergence in \(\mathbb{C}\)

A complex series \(\displaystyle \sum z_n\) is said to be absolutely convergent if the series

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |z_n| \]

converges, where

\[ |z_n| = \sqrt{(\Re z_n)^2 + (\Im z_n)^2}. \]

If \(\sum |z_n|\) converges, then the complex series \(\sum z_n\) also converges. In other words,

\[ \text{absolute convergence} \ \Longrightarrow \ \text{convergence}. \]

Moreover, any standard convergence test for real, non-negative series may be applied to the series \(\sum |z_n|\).

Worked Examples

Example - Ratio Test

Test

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(1+i)^n}{2^n}. \]

Here

\[ \rho = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left|\frac{(1+i)^{n+1}/2^{n+1}}{(1+i)^n/2^n}\right| = \left|\frac{1+i}{2}\right| = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} < 1. \]

Thus the series converges absolutely (and hence converges).

Example - Alternating parts

Test

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{i^n}{\sqrt{n}}. \]

The terms cycle through \(i,-1,-i,1,\dots\). Real part: \(\sum \frac{\pm 1}{\sqrt{n}}\) alternating — converges by the alternating series test. Imaginary part: \(\sum \frac{\pm 1}{\sqrt{n}}\) alternating — also converges. Hence the series converges (conditionally, not absolutely).

Example - Geometric Series
\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty (re^{i\theta})^n \]

converges iff \(|r|<1\), in which case

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty (re^{i\theta})^n = \frac{1}{1-re^{i\theta}}. \]

Quick Facts

For the ratio test applied to \(\sum z_n\):

  • If \(\rho < 1\), the series converges absolutely.

  • If \(\rho > 1\), the series diverges.

  • If \(\rho = 1\), the test is inconclusive — real and imaginary parts must be checked separately.

Complex Power Series: Disk of Convergence

Definition - Complex Power Series

A complex power series centered at \(z_0\) has the form

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (z - z_0)^n, \qquad c_n \in \mathbb{C}. \]

It converges for all \(z\) within some disk centered at \(z_0\), and diverges for all \(z\) outside that disk.

Radius and Disk of Convergence

Theorem - Radius of Convergence (Ratio Test)

If

\[ \rho = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left| \frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n} \right| \quad \text{exists, then} \quad R = \frac{1}{\rho}, \]

with conventions:

\[ \rho = 0 \Rightarrow R = \infty, \qquad \rho = \infty \Rightarrow R = 0. \]

A complex power series converges absolutely when

\[ |z - z_0| < R, \]

and diverges when

\[ |z - z_0| > R. \]

The set

\[ \{\, z \in \mathbb{C} : |z - z_0| < R \,\} \]

is called the disk of convergence. On the boundary \(|z-z_0| = R\), convergence must be tested separately.

Example - Maclaurin Series for \(\log(1+z)\)
\[ \log(1+z) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} z^n. \]

Ratio test:

\[ \rho = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^{n+2} z^{n+1} /(n+1)}{(-1)^{n+1} z^n/n} \right| = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{|z| \, n}{n+1} = |z|. \]

Thus \(R = 1\), so the disk of convergence is \(|z|<1\).

Example - Shifted Center
\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(z + 1 - i)^n}{3^n}. \]

Ratio test:

\[ \rho = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left| \frac{(z+1-i)^{n+1}/3^{n+1}}{(z+1-i)^n / 3^n} \right| = \frac{|z+1-i|}{3}. \]

Thus convergence requires \(|z+1-i| < 3\). The disk is centered at \(z_0 = -1 + i\) with radius \(R = 3\).

Remark - Properties of the Disk of Convergence

  • If \(R = \infty\), the power series converges for all \(z \in \mathbb{C}\). In this case, the function defined by the series is called entire.

  • If \(R = 0\), the series converges only at the center \(z = z_0\).

  • For all \(z\) with \(|z - z_0| < R\), convergence is uniform on compact subsets of the disk. Term-by-term differentiation and integration are therefore valid within this region (see Theorem in Section 2.5).

Euler's Formula

Theorem - Euler's Formula

For any real angle \(\theta\),

\[ e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta. \]

This identity connects the complex exponential with the trigonometric functions and plays a central role in oscillations, waves, quantum mechanics, and electrical engineering.

Proof Using Power Series

Remark - Strategy of the Proof

We will:

  1. Start from the power series definition of \(e^{x}\) and substitute \(x = i\theta\).

  2. Expand \((i\theta)^n\) and separate the terms with even and odd powers of \(n\).

  3. Simplify powers of \(i\) and identify the real and imaginary parts with the known series for \(\cos\theta\) and \(\sin\theta\).

Step 1. Start from the series for \(e^{x}\).

For any real (or complex) number \(x\),

\[ e^{x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!}. \]

Substitute \(x = i\theta\) (with \(\theta \in \mathbb{R}\)):

\[ e^{i\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!} = \frac{(i\theta)^0}{0!} + \frac{(i\theta)^1}{1!} + \frac{(i\theta)^2}{2!} + \frac{(i\theta)^3}{3!} + \frac{(i\theta)^4}{4!} + \cdots. \]

Step 2. Write out the first few terms explicitly.

\[ \begin{aligned} e^{i\theta} &= 1 + i\theta + \frac{(i\theta)^2}{2!} + \frac{(i\theta)^3}{3!} + \frac{(i\theta)^4}{4!} + \frac{(i\theta)^5}{5!} + \cdots. \end{aligned} \]

Now expand the powers of \(i\):

\[ i^0 = 1,\quad i^1 = i,\quad i^2 = -1,\quad i^3 = -i,\quad i^4 = 1,\quad i^5 = i,\ \dots \]

So we obtain:

\[ \begin{aligned} e^{i\theta} &= 1 + i\theta + \frac{i^2\theta^2}{2!} + \frac{i^3\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{i^4\theta^4}{4!} + \frac{i^5\theta^5}{5!} + \cdots \\[4pt] &= 1 + i\theta + \frac{(-1)\theta^2}{2!} + \frac{(-i)\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{(1)\theta^4}{4!} + \frac{(i)\theta^5}{5!} + \cdots. \end{aligned} \]

Step 3. Group real and imaginary terms.

Group all the terms without \(i\) (real part) and all the terms with a factor \(i\) (imaginary part):

\[ \begin{aligned} e^{i\theta} &= \underbrace{\Biggl(1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2!} + \frac{\theta^4}{4!} - \frac{\theta^6}{6!} + \cdots\Biggr)}_{\text{real part}} + i\,\underbrace{\Biggl(\theta - \frac{\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{\theta^5}{5!} - \frac{\theta^7}{7!} + \cdots\Biggr)}_{\text{imaginary part}}. \end{aligned} \]

Thus we can write

\[ e^{i\theta} = A(\theta) + i\,B(\theta), \]

where

\[ A(\theta) = 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2!} + \frac{\theta^4}{4!} - \frac{\theta^6}{6!} + \cdots, \]
\[ B(\theta) = \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{\theta^5}{5!} - \frac{\theta^7}{7!} + \cdots. \]

Step 4. Recall the power series for \(\cos\theta\) and \(\sin\theta\).

From earlier results on Maclaurin series:

\[ \cos\theta = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \theta^{2n}}{(2n)!} = 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2!} + \frac{\theta^4}{4!} - \frac{\theta^6}{6!} + \cdots, \]
\[ \sin\theta = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \theta^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{\theta^5}{5!} - \frac{\theta^7}{7!} + \cdots. \]

Comparing term by term, we see

\[ A(\theta) = \cos\theta, \qquad B(\theta) = \sin\theta. \]

Therefore,

\[ e^{i\theta} = A(\theta) + i B(\theta) = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta, \]

which completes the proof.

Geometric Meaning

Euler’s formula shows that \(e^{i\theta}\) lies on the unit circle in the complex plane:

\[ |e^{i\theta}| = 1, \qquad \arg(e^{i\theta}) = \theta. \]

Thus multiplying by \(e^{i\theta}\) corresponds to a rotation by angle \(\theta\) about the origin.

Applications of Euler's Formula

Remark - Polar Form

Any nonzero complex number \(z\) can be written as

\[ z = r e^{i\theta}, \qquad r = |z|,\ \theta = \arg(z). \]

This is the polar form of \(z\).

Remark - Multiplication and Division

If \(z_1 = r_1 e^{i\theta_1}\) and \(z_2 = r_2 e^{i\theta_2}\), then

\[ z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}, \qquad \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{r_1}{r_2} e^{i(\theta_1-\theta_2)}. \]

Thus multiplication adds angles, and division subtracts angles.

Remark - Roots of Unity

The \(n\)-th roots of \(1\) are

\[ z_k = e^{i\frac{2\pi k}{n}}, \qquad k = 0,1,\dots,n-1, \]

which are equally spaced points on the unit circle.

Example

Write \(1+i\) in polar form.

We calculate:

\[ r = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{1}{1}\right) = \frac{\pi}{4}. \]

Thus:

\[ 1+i = \sqrt{2}\, e^{i\pi/4}. \]
Example

Compute \((1+i)^8\).

Using Example 9.1:

\[ (1+i)^8 = (\sqrt{2}\, e^{i\pi/4})^8 = (\sqrt{2})^8 e^{i 8\pi/4} = 16 e^{i 2\pi} = 16. \]
Example

Find the cube roots of \(8e^{i\pi}\).

Here \(r=8\), \(\theta=\pi\). The cube roots are:

\[ z_k = 2 e^{i(\pi + 2\pi k)/3}, \quad k=0,1,2. \]

So the roots are:

\[ 2e^{i\pi/3}, \quad 2e^{i\pi}, \quad 2e^{i5\pi/3}. \]

Elementary Functions of Complex Numbers

We extend familiar real-valued functions such as the exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric functions to complex inputs \(z \in \mathbb{C}\).

These generalizations are most naturally defined using their power series expansions, since power series:

  • converge for complex numbers in the same region as for real numbers,

  • preserve key algebraic identities (e.g., \(e^{z+w} = e^z e^w\)),

  • allow term-by-term differentiation and integration inside their disks of convergence.

Polynomials and Rational Functions

Polynomials and quotients of polynomials in \(z\) are handled by direct substitution and algebra.

Example

Take for example \( f(z)=\dfrac{z^2+1}{z-3} \). Find \( f(i-2) \).

\[ f(i-2)=\frac{(i-2)^2+1}{(i-2)-3} = \frac{-1-4i+4+1}{\,i-5\,} = \frac{4-4i}{\,i-5\,}. \]

Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate \(-i-5\):

\[ \frac{4-4i}{\,i-5\,} \cdot\frac{-i-5}{-i-5} =\frac{(4-4i)(-i-5)}{(i-5)(-i-5)}. \]

Expand the numerator and simplify:

\[ (4-4i)(-i-5) =4(-i-5)-4i(-i-5) =-4i-20+\underbrace{4i^2}_{-4}+20i = (-24)+16i. \]

Denominator:

\[ (i-5)(-i-5) = -i^2-5i+5i+25=1+25=26. \]

Therefore

\[ f(i-2)=\frac{-24+16i}{26}=\boxed{\frac{8i-12}{13}}. \]

The Exponential Function

Definition - Complex Exponential

The exponential function for \(z \in \mathbb{C}\) is defined by the power series

\[ e^{z} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{z^n}{n!} = 1 + z + \frac{z^2}{2!} + \frac{z^3}{3!} + \cdots. \]

This series converges for all complex numbers \(z\).

Theorem - Main Properties of \(e^z\)

For all complex numbers \(z, z_1, z_2 \in \mathbb{C}\),

  1. \(e^{z_1+z_2} = e^{z_1}e^{z_2}\),

  2. \(\dfrac{d}{dz}(e^{z}) = e^{z}\),

  3. If \(z = x + iy\), then \(e^z = e^x(\cos y + i\sin y)\).

(a) Show that \(e^{z_1+z_2} = e^{z_1}e^{z_2}\).

Expand each exponential:

\[ e^{z_1+z_2} = 1 + (z_1+z_2) + \frac{(z_1+z_2)^2}{2!} + \frac{(z_1+z_2)^3}{3!} + \cdots. \]

Similarly,

\[ e^{z_1} e^{z_2} = \left(1 + z_1 + \frac{z_1^2}{2!} + \frac{z_1^3}{3!} + \cdots\right) \left(1 + z_2 + \frac{z_2^2}{2!} + \frac{z_2^3}{3!} + \cdots\right). \]

Multiplying out gives:

\[ e^{z_1}e^{z_2} = 1 + (z_1+z_2) + \frac{z_1^2 + 2z_1z_2 + z_2^2}{2!} + \cdots \]

Each coefficient matches term-by-term the expansion of \(e^{z_1+z_2}\). Thus,

\[ \boxed{e^{z_1+z_2} = e^{z_1}e^{z_2}}. \]

(b) Show that \(\dfrac{d}{dz}(e^{z}) = e^{z}\).

Differentiate term-by-term:

\[ \frac{d}{dz} e^{z} = \frac{d}{dz}\left( 1 + z + \frac{z^2}{2!} + \frac{z^3}{3!} + \cdots \right) = 0 + 1 + \frac{2z}{2!} + \frac{3z^2}{3!} + \cdots. \]

Simplify:

\[ \frac{d}{dz} e^{z} = 1 + z + \frac{z^2}{2!} + \frac{z^3}{3!} + \cdots = e^{z}. \]
\[ \boxed{\dfrac{d}{dz}(e^{z}) = e^{z}}. \]

(c) Simplify \(e^z\) into real and imaginary parts.

Let \(z = x + iy\). Then

\[ e^{z} = e^{x+iy} = e^{x}e^{iy}. \]

Using Euler’s formula:

\[ e^{iy} = \cos y + i\sin y. \]

So,

\[ \boxed{e^{x+iy} = e^x(\cos y + i\sin y)}. \]
Example

Compute \( e^{2+i}\,e^{-1+3i} \) without expanding series.

Solution.

\[ e^{2+i}\,e^{-1+3i}=e^{(2+i)+(-1+3i)}=e^{1+4i} =e^{1}\big(\cos 4 + i\sin 4\big). \]

Powers and Roots of Complex Numbers

Using the rules for multiplication and division of complex numbers in polar form, let

\[ z = r e^{i\theta}, \qquad r = |z| \ge 0,\ \theta = \arg(z). \]
Theorem - Powers of a Complex Number

For any integer \(n\),

\begin{equation} z^{\,n} = (r e^{i\theta})^{\,n} = r^{\,n} e^{\,i n \theta}. \end{equation}

In words: to take the \(n\)-th power of a complex number, raise the modulus to the \(n\)-th power and multiply the angle by \(n\).

When \(r=1\), this becomes De Moivre’s formula:

\begin{equation} \boxed{ (e^{i\theta})^{\,n} = (\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^{\,n} = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta). } \tag{10.2} \end{equation}
Theorem - \(n\)-th Roots of a Complex Number

The \(n\)-th root of \(z\), written \(z^{1/n}\), is a complex number whose \(n\)-th power is \(z\). From \((10.1)\), the \(n\)-th roots of \(z = r e^{i\theta}\) are

\begin{equation} \boxed{ z^{1/n} = \bigl(r e^{i\theta} e^{i(2m\pi)}\bigr)^{1/n} = r^{1/n} e^{\,i(\theta + 2m\pi)/n} = \sqrt[n]{r}\!\left( \cos\frac{\theta + 2m\pi}{n} + i\sin\frac{\theta + 2m\pi}{n} \right), } \end{equation}

where \( m = 0,1,\dots,n-1 \)

Example

Compute \((1+i)^8\).

Write

\[ 1 + i = \sqrt{2}\,e^{i\pi/4}. \]

Then

\[ (1+i)^8 = (\sqrt{2})^8 e^{i\,8\pi/4} = 16\,e^{i2\pi} = 16. \]
Example

Compute \((-\sqrt{3}+i)^3\).

Here

\[ r = \sqrt{(-\sqrt{3})^2 + 1^2} = 2, \qquad \theta = \arg(-\sqrt{3}+i) = \tfrac{5\pi}{6}. \]

Thus

\[ (-\sqrt{3}+i)^3 = 2^{3} e^{i\,3(5\pi/6)} = 8\,e^{i\,5\pi/2} = 8\,e^{i(\pi/2)} = 8i. \]
Example

Fourth roots of \(-16\).

We write

\[ -16 = 16\,e^{i\pi}. \]

With \(n=4\), the roots are

\[ w_k = 16^{1/4}\,e^{\,i(\pi+2\pi k)/4} = 2\,e^{\,i(\pi/4+\pi k/2)}, \qquad k=0,1,2,3. \]

Explicitly:

\[ 2e^{i\pi/4} = \sqrt{2}(1+i),\quad 2e^{i3\pi/4} = \sqrt{2}(-1+i),\quad 2e^{i5\pi/4} = \sqrt{2}(-1-i),\quad 2e^{i7\pi/4} = \sqrt{2}(1-i). \]
Example

Cube roots of \(8e^{i\pi}\).

Here \(r = 8\), \(\theta = \pi\), \(n=3\). Thus

\[ w_k = 2\,e^{\,i(\pi+2\pi k)/3}, \qquad k=0,1,2, \]

i.e.

\[ 2e^{i\pi/3},\quad 2e^{i\pi},\quad 2e^{i5\pi/3}. \]
Example - 5

Solve \(w^5=1\) (the 5th roots of unity).

We write

\[ 1 = e^{i\cdot 0}. \]

Then the solutions are

\[ w_k = e^{\,i(0+2\pi k)/5} = e^{\,i\,2\pi k/5}, \qquad k=0,1,2,3,4. \]

These five points form a regular pentagon on the unit circle.

Trigonometric Functions of a Complex Variable

Recall that Euler’s formula gives

\[ e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta, \qquad e^{-i\theta} = \cos\theta - i\sin\theta. \]

Adding and subtracting these expressions,

\[ \cos\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2}, \qquad \sin\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{2i}. \]
Theorem - Complex Sine and Cosine

For any complex number \(z = x + iy \in \mathbb{C}\),

\[ \boxed{ \sin z = \frac{e^{iz} - e^{-iz}}{2i}, \qquad \cos z = \frac{e^{iz} + e^{-iz}}{2}. } \]
Remark - Extension to Complex Domain

These formulas extend the real trigonometric functions to complex arguments while preserving their power series definitions and their analytic properties.

Example

  1. \(\displaystyle \cos(2i)=\frac{e^{i(2i)}+e^{-i(2i)}}{2} =\frac{e^{-2}+e^{2}}{2}\equiv \cosh(2)=3.762\ldots\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \sin\!\Big(\frac{\pi}{2}+i\ln(2)\Big)\)

    \[ \begin{aligned} \sin\!\Big(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+i\ln(2)\Big) &=\frac{e^{\,i(\pi/2+i\ln(2))}-e^{-\,i(\pi/2+i\ln(2))}}{2i}\\[2mm] &=\frac{e^{\,i\pi/2-\ln(2)}-e^{-\,i\pi/2+\ln(2)}}{2i}\\[2mm] &=\frac{i\,e^{-\ln|2|}+\,i\,e^{\ln(2)}}{2i} \qquad(\text{since }e^{i\pi/2}=i,\ e^{-i\pi/2}=-i)\\[2mm] &=\frac{i\cdot\frac{1}{2}+2i}{2i} =\Big(\frac{1}{2}+2\Big)\frac{1}{2} =\frac{1}{4}+1=\boxed{\frac{5}{4}}. \end{aligned} \]
Example - Prove that

\(\sin^{2}(z)+\cos^{2}(z)=1\).

\[ \sin^{2}(z)=\Big(\frac{e^{iz}-e^{-iz}}{2i}\Big)^{2} =\frac{e^{2iz}+e^{-2iz}-2}{-4}, \qquad \cos^{2}(z)=\Big(\frac{e^{iz}+e^{-iz}}{2}\Big)^{2} =\frac{e^{2iz}+e^{-2iz}+2}{4}. \]
\[ \Rightarrow\quad \sin^{2}(z)+\cos^{2}(z)=1. \]
Example - Show that

\(\dfrac{d}{dz}\cos(z)=-\sin(z)\).

\[ \frac{d}{dz}\Big(\frac{e^{iz}+e^{-iz}}{2}\Big) =\frac{i\,e^{iz}-\,i\,e^{-iz}}{2} = -\,\frac{e^{iz}-e^{-iz}}{2i} = -\,\sin(z). \]

Hyperbolic Functions

Assume \(z = iy\) is purely imaginary. Recall the complex definitions:

\[ \sin z = \frac{e^{iz} - e^{-iz}}{2i}, \qquad \cos z = \frac{e^{iz} + e^{-iz}}{2}. \]

Relation with Imaginary Arguments

Compute:

\[ \sin(iy) = \frac{e^{i(iy)} - e^{-i(iy)}}{2i} = \frac{e^{-y} - e^{y}}{2i} = \frac{1}{i}\cdot \frac{e^{-y} - e^{y}}{2} = i\,\frac{e^{y} - e^{-y}}{2} \equiv i\sinh(y), \]

and

\[ \cos(iy) = \frac{e^{i(iy)} + e^{-i(iy)}}{2} = \frac{e^{-y} + e^{y}}{2} \equiv \cosh(y). \]
Theorem - Relation Between Trigonometric and Hyperbolic Functions

For any real number \(y\),

\[ \boxed{\sin(iy) = i\sinh(y)}, \qquad \boxed{\cos(iy) = \cosh(y)}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \boxed{\sinh(iy) = i\sin(y)}, \qquad \boxed{\cosh(iy) = \cos(y)}. \]
Definition - Hyperbolic Functions

For any complex number \(z \in \mathbb{C}\), define

\[ \sinh(z) = \frac{e^{z} - e^{-z}}{2}, \qquad \cosh(z) = \frac{e^{z} + e^{-z}}{2}. \]

From these:

\[ \tanh(z) = \frac{\sinh(z)}{\cosh(z)}, \qquad \operatorname{coth}(z) = \frac{\cosh(z)}{\sinh(z)}, \]
\[ \operatorname{sech}(z) = \frac{1}{\cosh(z)}, \qquad \operatorname{csch}(z) = \frac{1}{\sinh(z)}. \]
Example

Show that \(\cosh^2(z)-\sinh^2(z)=1\)

\[ \cosh^2(z)=\Big(\frac{e^{z}+e^{-z}}{2}\Big)^2 =\frac{e^{2z}+e^{-2z}+2}{4}, \]
\[ \sinh^2(z)=\Big(\frac{e^{z}-e^{-z}}{2}\Big)^2 =\frac{e^{2z}+e^{-2z}-2}{4}. \]

Hence

\[ \cosh^2(z)-\sinh^2(z)=1. \]

Logarithms of Complex Numbers

If \(z = e^\omega\), then we define the complex logarithm by \(\ln(z) = \omega\).

Logarithm of a Product

Let \(z_1 = e^{\omega_1}\) and \(z_2 = e^{\omega_2}\). Then

\[ z_1 z_2 = e^{\omega_1} e^{\omega_2} = e^{\omega_1 + \omega_2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \ln(z_1 z_2) = \omega_1 + \omega_2 = \ln(z_1) + \ln(z_2). \]
Definition - Complex Logarithm

For a complex number \(z = re^{i\theta}\) with \(r = |z| > 0\),

\[ \ln(z) = \ln\big(re^{i\theta}\big) = \ln(r) + \ln\big(e^{i\theta}\big) = \ln r + i\theta. \]

Because the angle \(\theta\) is not unique (adding \(2\pi\) leaves the same point), the logarithm is a multivalued function:

\[ \ln(z) = \ln r + i(\theta + 2\pi k), \qquad k \in \mathbb{Z}. \]
Remark - Principal Value

We define the principal branch of the logarithm by restricting the argument:

\[ \boxed{ \mathrm{Ln}(z) = \ln r + i\Theta, \quad \text{where } -\pi < \Theta \le \pi. } \]

Here \(\Theta = \arg(z)\) is the principal argument.

Example
  1. \[ \ln(-1)=\ln\!\big(e^{i(2n+1)\pi}\big)=i(2n+1)\pi,\qquad n=\pm1,\pm2,\ldots \]

    Hence

    \[ \ln(-1)=\pm i\pi,\ \pm i3\pi,\ \pm i5\pi,\ldots \quad\text{and}\quad \mathrm{Ln}(-1)=i\pi. \]
  2. \[ \ln(1+i)=\ln(\sqrt{2})+\bigg(\frac{\pi}{4}+2n\pi\bigg)i,\qquad n\in\mathbb{Z}. \]

Complex Roots and Powers

Consider \(\ln(a^{b})=b\ln(a)\Rightarrow a^{b}=e^{\,b\ln(a)}\).

Example

  1. Find all the values of \(i^{-2i}\):

    \[ i^{-2i}=e^{-2i\ln(i)} =e^{-2i\,[\,\ln(1)+(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+2\pi n)i\,]} =e^{\,2(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+2\pi n)} =e^{\,\pi+4\pi n},\qquad n\in\mathbb{Z}. \]
