Chapter 4

Partial Differentiation

Introduction

In many physical problems, we encounter functions that depend on more than one variable, e.g.,

\[ f = f(x,y,z,\dots). \]

The partial derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) means differentiating \(f\) with respect to \(x\) while treating all the other variables as constants. We denote this as

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}. \]
Definition - Partial Derivative

Let \(f(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)\) be a function of several variables. The partial derivative of \(f\) with respect to the variable \(x_i\) is defined by

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} = \lim_{\Delta x_i \to 0} \frac{ f(x_1, \dots, x_i + \Delta x_i, \dots, x_n) - f(x_1, \dots, x_i, \dots, x_n) }{\Delta x_i}. \]

All other variables are held constant during the differentiation.

Notation

There are multiple notations for partial derivatives. For first-order:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = f_x. \]

For higher-order derivatives:

\[ \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x^2} = f_{xx}, \qquad \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = f_{xy}, \qquad \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial y^2} = f_{yy}. \]
Example - Partial Derivatives of a Multivariable Function

Let

\[ f(x,y) = x^3y - e^{xy}. \]

First-order partial derivatives:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 3x^2y - y e^{xy}, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^3 - x e^{xy}. \]

Second-order partial derivatives:

\[ \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x^2} = 6xy - y^2 e^{xy}, \qquad \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial y^2} = -x^2 e^{xy}. \]
\[ \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = 3x^2 - e^{xy} - xy e^{xy}, \qquad \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} = 3x^2 - e^{xy} - xy e^{xy}. \]

Conclusion: Mixed partials agree:

\[ \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial y \partial x}, \]

(as long as \(f\) is sufficiently smooth — Clairaut's Theorem).

Remark

For sufficiently smooth functions, the mixed derivatives are equal:

\[ \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial y \partial x}. \]

This is known as Clairaut’s theorem.

Example - Change of Variables and Partial Derivatives

Let

\[ z = x^2 - y^2, \qquad x = r\cos\theta,\quad y = r\sin\theta. \]

Then, using \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\), we can rewrite \(z\) in different but equivalent forms and compute \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\) while holding different variables constant.

1) Expressing \(z\) in terms of \(r\) and \(\theta\):

\[ z = r^2\cos^2\theta - r^2\sin^2\theta = r^2 \cos(2\theta). \]

Holding \(\theta\) constant:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\right)_{\theta} = 2r(\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta) = 2r\cos(2\theta). \]

2) Expressing \(z\) in terms of \(x\) and \(r\):

\[ z = 2x^2 - (x^2 + y^2) = 2x^2 - r^2. \]

Holding \(x\) constant:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\right)_{x} = -2r. \]

3) Expressing \(z\) in terms of \(y\) and \(r\):

\[ z = (x^2 + y^2) - 2y^2 = r^2 - 2y^2. \]

Holding \(y\) constant:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\right)_{y} = 2r. \]

Important: Each derivative is different because a different quantity is held fixed. Thus, we write:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\right)_{\theta}, \qquad \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\right)_{x}, \qquad \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\right)_{y}, \]

to remove ambiguity in multivariable calculus.

Applications in Thermodynamics

Partial derivatives play a central role in thermodynamics because most physical quantities depend on more than one variable. For example, temperature \(T\), pressure \(p\), volume \(V\), and entropy \(S\) are related through equations of state and energy relations. To describe how one variable changes while others are held constant, we use partial derivatives with explicit subscripts.

For example, the temperature can be expressed as a function of pressure and volume,

\[ T = T(p,V), \]

or as a function of pressure and internal energy,

\[ T = T(p,U). \]

In these cases, we encounter derivatives such as

\[ \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial p}\right)_V, \qquad \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial p}\right)_U, \quad \dots \]

  • \(\displaystyle \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial p}\right)_V\): Measures how the temperature changes with pressure at constant volume. Physically, this corresponds to compressing or expanding a system while keeping the volume fixed, and observing how the temperature must change.

  • \(\displaystyle \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial p}\right)_U\): Measures how the temperature changes with pressure at constant internal energy. This derivative is related to adiabatic processes, since keeping \(U\) constant means no net change in the system's internal energy. It indicates how pressure variations influence temperature when the system cannot exchange heat or work to change its internal energy.

Remark

The subscript indicates which variables are kept constant during differentiation. For example:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial p}\right)_V \quad \text{means ``change in $T$ with respect to $p$ while keeping $V$ fixed.''} \]

This removes ambiguity and prevents misinterpretation of derivatives.

Power Series in Two Variables

Just as a function of a single variable can be expressed as a power series expansion, functions of two variables can also be expanded into a series about a point \((a,b)\).

Example - Series Expansion of \(\sin(x)\cos(y)\)

Use the power series

\[ \sin(x) = x - \frac{x^3}{3!} + \frac{x^5}{5!} - \cdots, \qquad \cos(y) = 1 - \frac{y^2}{2!} + \frac{y^4}{4!} - \cdots. \]

Multiply the expansions:

\[ \sin(x)\cos(y) = \left(x - \frac{x^3}{3!} + \cdots\right) \left(1 - \frac{y^2}{2!} + \cdots\right). \]

Collecting lowest-order terms:

\[ = x - \frac{x^3}{3!} - \frac{x y^2}{2!} + \cdots \]

Thus, the (truncated) power-series expansion is:

\[ \boxed{\sin(x)\cos(y) = x - \frac{x^3}{6} - \frac{xy^2}{2} + \cdots} \]
Example - Series Expansion of \(\ln(1+x-y)\)

Start with the known expansion (for \(|z|<1\)):

\[ \ln(1+z) = z - \frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{z^3}{3} - \cdots. \]

Let \(z = x - y\):

\[ \ln(1+x-y) = (x-y) - \frac{(x-y)^2}{2} + \frac{(x-y)^3}{3} - \cdots. \]

Expand:

\[ = (x-y) - \frac{x^2 - 2xy + y^2}{2} + \frac{x^3 - 3x^2y + 3xy^2 - y^3}{3} - \cdots \]

Simplify:

\[ \boxed{ \ln(1+x-y) = x - y - \frac{x^2}{2} + xy - \frac{y^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3} - x^2y + xy^2 - \frac{y^3}{3} + \cdots } \]

General Expansion

In general, a sufficiently smooth function \(f(x,y)\) can be expanded in a power series about a point \((a,b)\) as

\[ f(x,y) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \left((x-a)\frac{\partial}{\partial X} + (y-b)\frac{\partial}{\partial X}\right)^n \left.f(X,Y)\right|_{(a,b)}. \]

This is the multivariable Taylor series expansion.

Taylor Expansion Approach

Instead of multiplying series, we can also compute the expansion of

\[ f(x,y) = \sin(x)\cos(y) \]

directly using the two-variable Taylor series about \((0,0)\):

\[ \begin{aligned} f(x,y) &= f(0,0) + \left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial X}\right|_{(0,0)} x + \left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial Y}\right|_{(0,0)} y \\[6pt] &\quad + \frac{1}{2!}\Bigg[ \left.\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial X^{2}}\right|_{(0,0)} x^2 + 2 \left.\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial X\,\partial Y}\right|_{(0,0)} xy + \left.\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial Y^{2}}\right|_{(0,0)} y^2 \Bigg] + \cdots \end{aligned} \]

Step 1. Zeroth order

\[ f(0,0) = \sin(0)\cos(0) = 0. \]

Step 2. First order

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial X} = \cos(X)\cos(Y), \quad \left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial X}\right|_{(0,0)} = 1, \]
\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial Y} = -\sin(X)\sin(Y), \quad \left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial Y}\right|_{(0,0)} = 0. \]

So the linear contribution is \(x\).

Step 3. Second order

At \((0,0)\), all second derivatives vanish, so there is no quadratic contribution.

Step 4. Third order

\[ \left.\frac{\partial^{3} f}{\partial X^{3}}\right|_{(0,0)} = -1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad -\frac{x^3}{3!}, \]

Result

Collecting terms:

\[ \sin(x)\cos(y) = x - \frac{x^3}{3!} + \cdots \]
Remark

This matches the result obtained earlier by multiplying the single-variable series. Using the multivariable Taylor expansion provides a systematic way to compute higher-order terms.

Total Differentials

Review: One Variable

If \(y = f(x)\), then

\[ y' = \frac{d y}{d x} = \frac{d f(x)}{d x}. \]

By definition,

\[ \frac{d y}{d x} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}. \]

Hence, for small \(\Delta x\),

\[ dx = \Delta x, \qquad dy = y' \, dx. \]

Two Variables

If \(f = f(x,y)\), then the total differential is

\[ df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} dy. \]

General Case

For a function of many variables,

\[ f = f(x,y,z,\dots), \]

the total differential is

\[ df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} dy + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} dz + \cdots \]
Remark

The total differential expresses the approximate change in \(f\) resulting from small changes in its independent variables.

Chain Rule: Differentiating a Function of a Function

The chain rule extends naturally to functions of functions. If a variable depends on another, which in turn depends on a third, we can differentiate by following the chain of dependencies.

Theorem - Chain Rule for Composite Functions

If \(y = y(u)\) and \(u = u(x)\), then the derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(x\) is

\[ \boxed{\frac{d y}{d x} = \frac{d y}{d u}\,\frac{d u}{d x}}. \]

More generally, if

\[ u = u(x,y), \qquad x = x(t), \qquad y = y(t), \]

then

\[ \boxed{\frac{d u}{d t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\,\frac{d x}{d t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\,\frac{d y}{d t}}. \]

For functions depending on multiple variables,

\[ \boxed{ \frac{d u}{d t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\,\frac{d x}{d t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\,\frac{d y}{d t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\,\frac{d z}{d t} + \cdots } \]

where the sum includes a term for each independent variable.

Example

Let

\[ y = \ln(u), \qquad u = \sin(v), \qquad v = 2x. \]

Find \(\frac{d y}{d x}\).

Solution
\[ \frac{d y}{d x} = \frac{d y}{d u}\,\frac{d u}{d v}\,\frac{d v}{d x}. \]

Compute the derivatives step by step:

\[ \frac{d y}{d u} = \frac{1}{u}, \qquad \frac{d u}{d v} = \cos(v), \qquad \frac{d v}{d x} = 2. \]

Thus,

\[ \frac{d y}{d x} = \frac{1}{u}\cos(v)\cdot 2 = \frac{2\cos(v)}{u}. \]

Since \(u=\sin(v)\),

\[ \boxed{\frac{d y}{d x} = \frac{2\cos(v)}{\sin(v)} = 2\cot(v) = 2\cot(2x).} \]
Example

Let

\[ z = x y, \qquad x = 2t^2, \qquad y = \sin(t). \]

Find \(\frac{d z}{d t}\).

Solution
\[ \frac{d z}{d t} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{d x}{d t} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{d y}{d t}. \]

Since

\[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = y, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = x, \]

we get

\[ \frac{d z}{d t} = y\,\frac{d x}{d t} + x\,\frac{d y}{d t}. \]

Now compute the derivatives:

\[ \frac{d x}{d t} = 4t, \qquad \frac{d y}{d t} = \cos(t). \]

Therefore,

\[ \frac{d z}{d t} = \sin(t)(4t) + (2t^2)\cos(t). \]
\[ \boxed{\frac{d z}{d t} = 4t\sin(t) + 2t^2\cos(t).} \]
Remark

The chain rule allows us to differentiate composite functions systematically, even when variables are nested or interdependent.

Implicit Differentiation

Often, functions are defined implicitly rather than explicitly. For example, if \(x\) and \(t\) are related by

\[ x + e^x = t, \]

then \(x\) is an implicit function of \(t\).

First Derivative

Differentiate both sides of

\[ x + e^x = t \]

with respect to \(t\):

\[ \frac{d x}{d t} + e^x \frac{d x}{d t} = 1. \]

Thus,

\[ \frac{d x}{d t} = \frac{1}{1+e^x}. \]

Second Derivative

From the expression for \(\frac{d x}{d t}\), we differentiate again:

\[ \frac{d^{2} x}{d t^{2}} = \frac{d}{d t}\!\left[(1+e^x)^{-1}\right]. \]

Apply the chain rule:

\[ \frac{d^{2} x}{d t^{2}} = -(1+e^x)^{-2}\cdot e^x \frac{d x}{d t}. \]

Now substitute \(\frac{d x}{d t} = \dfrac{1}{1+e^x}\):

\[ \frac{d^{2} x}{d t^{2}} = -\frac{e^x}{(1+e^x)^3}. \]
Example

Find the equation of the tangent line to the curve

\[ x^3 - 3y^3 + xy + 2 = 0 \]

at the point \((1,2)\).

Solution

Differentiate implicitly with respect to \(x\):

\[ 3x^2 - 9y^2\,\frac{d y}{d x} + y + x\,\frac{d y}{d x} = 0. \]

Group the \(\frac{d y}{d x}\) terms:

\[ (x - 9y^2)\frac{d y}{d x} = -(3x^2 + y). \]

Solve for slope:

\[ \frac{d y}{d x} = \frac{-(3x^2 + y)}{x - 9y^2}. \]

At the point \((1,2)\):

\[ \frac{d y}{d x}\Big|_{(1,2)} = \frac{-(3(1)^2 + 2)}{1 - 9(2^2)} = \frac{-5}{-35} = \frac{1}{7}. \]

The tangent line with slope \(m = \frac{1}{7}\) at \((1,2)\):

\[ y - 2 = \frac{1}{7}(x - 1). \]

Simplifying:

\[ \boxed{x - 7y + 13 = 0.} \]
Remark

Implicit differentiation allows us to compute derivatives (and tangent lines) for curves not expressed explicitly as \(y=f(x)\).

More on Chain Rule

Theorem - General Chain Rule

Suppose

\[ u = u(x,y,z), \qquad x = x(s,t), \quad y = y(s,t), \quad z = z(s,t). \]

Then the chain rule gives

\[ \boxed{ \frac{\partial u}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}} \]
\[ \boxed{ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}} \]
Proof

Write the total differential of \(u=u(x,y,z)\):

\[ du = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\,dx + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\,dy + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\,dz. \]

Since \(x=x(s,t),\; y=y(s,t),\; z=z(s,t)\), their differentials are

\[ dx = \frac{\partial x}{\partial s}\,ds + \frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\,dt,\qquad dy = \frac{\partial y}{\partial s}\,ds + \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\,dt,\qquad dz = \frac{\partial z}{\partial s}\,ds + \frac{\partial z}{\partial t}\,dt. \]

Substitute into \(du\) and collect coefficients of \(ds\) and \(dt\):

\[ \begin{aligned} du &= \Big(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}\Big)\,ds \\ &\quad + \Big(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}\Big)\,dt. \end{aligned} \]

By the definition of partial derivatives of \(u\) as a function of \((s,t)\),

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial s},\qquad \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}. \]

Writing these two identities in vector form gives

\[ \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial u}{\partial s} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial z} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial s} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial t} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial y}{\partial s} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial z}{\partial s} & \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} \end{pmatrix}, \]

which is the desired matrix chain rule.

Example

Let

\[ z = xy, \qquad x = \sin(s+t), \qquad y = s - t. \]

We want to compute \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}\) and \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}\).

Solution

Step 1. Apply the Chain Rule

\[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}. \]

Step 2. Compute partial derivatives of \(z\)

Since \(z = xy\),

\[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = y, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = x. \]

Step 3. Compute derivatives of \(x\) and \(y\)

\[ x = \sin(s+t) \Rightarrow \frac{\partial x}{\partial s} = \cos(s+t), \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial t} = \cos(s+t), \]
\[ y = s - t \Rightarrow \frac{\partial y}{\partial s} = 1, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = -1. \]

Step 4. Substitute and simplify

\[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial s} = y\cos(s+t) + x, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = y\cos(s+t) - x. \]
\[ \boxed{ \frac{\partial z}{\partial s} = (s - t)\cos(s+t) + \sin(s+t), \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = (s - t)\cos(s+t) - \sin(s+t) } \]
Example

For

\[ z=x-y,\qquad x^2+y^2=t^2,\qquad x\sin t=ye^y, \]

find \(\frac{d z}{d t}=?\)

Solution

Step 1. Differentiate the constraints.

\[ 2x\,dx+2y\,dy=2t\,dt, \qquad \sin t\,dx+x\cos t\,dt=(y+1)e^y\,dy. \]

Step 2. Rearrange to a linear system for \(dx,dy\).

\[ x\,dx+y\,dy=t\,dt, \qquad \sin t\,dx-(y+1)e^y\,dy=-x\cos t\,dt. \]

Step 3. Solve for \(dx\) (and similarly \(dy\)) by determinants.

\[ dx =\frac{ \begin{vmatrix} t\,dt & y\\[4pt] - x\cos t\,dt & -(y+1)e^y \end{vmatrix} }{ \begin{vmatrix} x & y\\[4pt] \sin t & -(y+1)e^y \end{vmatrix} } =\frac{-t(y+1)e^y + xy\cos t}{-x(y+1)e^y - y\sin t}\,dt. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \frac{dx}{dt} =\frac{t(y+1)e^y - xy\cos t}{x(y+1)e^y + y\sin t}. \]

A parallel computation gives

\[ dy =\frac{ \begin{vmatrix} x & t\,dt \\[4pt] \sin t & -x\cos t\,dt \end{vmatrix} }{ \begin{vmatrix} x & y \\[4pt] \sin t & -(y+1)e^y \end{vmatrix} } =\frac{-x^{2}\cos t - t\sin t}{-x(y+1)e^y - y\sin t}\,dt. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \frac{dy}{dt} =\frac{x^{2}\cos t + t\sin t}{x(y+1)e^y + y\sin t}. \]

Since \(z = x - y\), we have

\[ \frac{dz}{dt}=\frac{dx}{dt}-\frac{dy}{dt}. \]
Example

Find \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}\) and \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}\) given

\[ z = x^2 + xy, \qquad x^2 + y^3 = st + 5, \qquad x^3 - y^2 = s^2 + t^2. \]
Solution

Step 1. Differential of \(z\)

We compute

\[ dz = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} dy = (2x+y)\,dx + x\,dy. \]

Step 2. Differentiating the constraints

From the two constraint equations, take differentials:

\[ 2x\,dx + 3y^2\,dy = s\,dt + t\,ds, \]
\[ 3x^2\,dx - 2y\,dy = 2s\,ds + 2t\,dt. \]

These form a linear system for \(dx\) and \(dy\) in terms of \(ds,dt\).

Step 3. Solve for \(dx,dy\)

We write in matrix form:

\[ \begin{pmatrix} 2x & 3y^2 \\[6pt] 3x^2 & -2y \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} dx \\[6pt] dy \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} s\,dt + t\,ds \\[6pt] 2s\,ds + 2t\,dt \end{pmatrix}. \]

Thus

\[ dx = \frac{ \begin{vmatrix} s\,dt+t\,ds & 3y^2 \\ 2s\,ds+2t\,dt & -2y \end{vmatrix} }{ \begin{vmatrix} 2x & 3y^2 \\ 3x^2 & -2y \end{vmatrix} }, \qquad dy = \frac{ \begin{vmatrix} 2x & s\,dt+t\,ds \\ 3x^2 & 2s\,ds+2t\,dt \end{vmatrix} }{ \begin{vmatrix} 2x & 3y^2 \\ 3x^2 & -2y \end{vmatrix} }. \]

Determinant denominator

\[ D= \begin{vmatrix} 2x & 3y^2\\ 3x^2 & -2y \end{vmatrix} =-4xy-9x^2y^2 = -(4xy+9x^2y^2). \]

Cramer's rule results simplified

\[ dx=\frac{-\big(2ys\,dt+2yt\,ds+6y^2s\,ds+6y^2t\,dt\big)}{D},\qquad dy=\frac{\,4xs\,ds+4xt\,dt-3x^2s\,dt-3x^2t\,ds\,}{D}. \]

Step 4. Substitution into \(dz\)

\[ dz=(2x+y)\,dx+x\,dy =\frac{N_s\,ds+N_t\,dt}{D}, \]

with

\[ \begin{aligned} N_s&=4sx^2-12sxy^2-6sy^3-3tx^3-4txy-2ty^2,\\ N_t&=-3sx^3-4sxy-2sy^2+4tx^2-12txy^2-6ty^3. \end{aligned} \]

Final partial derivatives (with positive denominator)

\[ \boxed{\; \frac{\partial z}{\partial s} =\frac{3t x^3+4txy+2t y^2-4s x^2+12sxy^2+6s y^3}{\,4xy+9x^2y^2\,} \;} \]
\[ \boxed{\; \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} =\frac{3s x^3+4sxy+2s y^2-4t x^2+12txy^2+6t y^3}{\,4xy+9x^2y^2\,} \;} \]
Example

Given the equations of Example 3, find \(\left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial z}\right)_{x}\).

Solution

Step 1. \(dx =0\) in Example 3

\[ dz = x\,dy. \]
\[ 3y^2\,dy = s\,dt + t\,ds, \]
\[ - 2y\,dy = 2s\,ds + 2t\,dt. \]

These form a linear system for \(ds\), \(dt\), and \(dy\). The system becomes:

\[ \begin{cases} x\,dy = dz, \\ t\,ds + s\,dt - 3y^2\,dy = 0, \\ 2s\,ds + 2t\,dt + 2y\,dy = 0. \end{cases} \]

Step 2. Solve using determinants

Writing as a determinant system, we get

\[ ds = \frac{ \begin{vmatrix} dz & 0 & x \\ 0 & s & -3y^2 \\ 0 & 2t & 2y \end{vmatrix} }{ \begin{vmatrix} 0 & 0 & x \\ t & s & -3y^2 \\ 2s & 2t & 2y \end{vmatrix} }. \]

Evaluating:

\[ ds = \frac{(2sy + 6ty^2)\,dz}{x(2t^2 - 2s^2)}. \]

Thus

\[ \left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial z}\right)_{x} = \frac{sy + 3ty^2}{x(t^2 - s^2)}. \]

Chain Rule in Polar and Rectangular Coordinates

Let \(x,y\) be rectangular coordinates and \(r,\theta\) be polar coordinates in a plane. The relations are

\[ x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta, \]

or equivalently

\[ r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \qquad \theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right). \]

We want to study derivatives such as \(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\), \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\), etc., and check whether they are reciprocals.

Step 1. Compute \(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\)

From

\[ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right), \]

we treat \(y\) as constant and differentiate:

\[ \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = \frac{-y/x^2}{1+(y/x)^2} = -\frac{y}{r^2}. \]

This is equation (7.7).

Step 2. Compute \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\)

From \(x = r\cos\theta\) we get

\[ \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta} = -r\sin\theta = -y. \]
Remark

Clearly,

\[ \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \neq \frac{1}{\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}}. \]

These derivatives are not reciprocals. The reason: \(\big(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\big)_y\) means \(y\) is held constant, whereas \(\big(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\big)_r\) means \(r\) is held constant. Since the conditions differ, they are not reciprocals.

Step 3. A reciprocal relation

Let’s compute

\[ \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\right)_y. \]

We use \(x = r\cos\theta\), \(y = r\sin\theta\), so with \(y\) constant:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\right)_y = y(-\csc^2\theta) = -\frac{y}{\sin^2\theta}. \]

Meanwhile,

\[ \left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\right)_y = -\frac{y}{r^2}. \]

Now check:

\[ \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\right)_y = \frac{1}{\left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\right)_y}. \]

So these are reciprocals.

Remark - Rule

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial v}\) and \(\frac{\partial v}{\partial u}\) are not usually reciprocals. They are reciprocals only if the set of independent variables (besides \(u,v\)) are the same in both cases.

Step 4. Using differentials

From \(\theta = \arctan(y/x)\) we can find

\[ d\theta = \frac{x\,dy - y\,dx}{r^2}. \]

From \(x = r\cos\theta\),

\[ dx = \cos\theta\,dr - r\sin\theta\,d\theta. \]

If \(y\) is constant (\(dy=0\)), then

\[ d\theta_y = -\frac{y}{r^2}\,dx_y, \]

so

\[ \left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\right)_y = \frac{d\theta_y}{dx_y} = -\frac{y}{r^2}. \]

By inversion,

\[ \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\right)_y = \frac{dx_y}{d\theta_y} = -\frac{r^2}{y}, \]

which is indeed the reciprocal.

Matrix notation

It is convenient to write

\[ \begin{pmatrix} dx \\ dy \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} dr \\ d\theta \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\[6pt] \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} dr \\ d\theta \end{pmatrix}. \]

Denote this Jacobian matrix as \(A\).

Then the inverse relation is

\[ \begin{pmatrix} dr \\ d\theta \end{pmatrix} = A^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} dx \\ dy \end{pmatrix}. \]

Carrying out the inversion, we obtain

\[ \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial r}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial r}{\partial y} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\[6pt] -\tfrac{1}{r}\sin\theta & \tfrac{1}{r}\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}. \]

Read off partial derivatives

Thus,

\[ \frac{\partial r}{\partial x} = \cos\theta = \frac{x}{r}, \quad \frac{\partial r}{\partial y} = \sin\theta = \frac{y}{r}, \]
\[ \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = -\frac{1}{r}\sin\theta = -\frac{y}{r^2}, \quad \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} = \frac{1}{r}\cos\theta = \frac{x}{r^2}. \]
Remark

Notice: although individual pairs like \(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\) are not reciprocals, the Jacobian matrices \( \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\[6pt] \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \) and \(\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\[6pt] -\tfrac{1}{r}\sin\theta & \tfrac{1}{r}\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}\) are inverses of each other.

Change of Variables: Laplace Equation in Polar Coordinates

We want to write the Laplace equation

\[ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x^{2}} + \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y^{2}} = 0 \]

in terms of polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\), where

\[ x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta. \]

Step 1. First Derivatives

Way 1: Chain rule form

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial r} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial r} = \cos\theta \,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + \sin\theta \,\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \]
\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta} = -r\sin\theta \,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + r\cos\theta \,\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}. \]

In matrix form,

\[ \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\[6pt] -r\sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\tfrac{\sin\theta}{r} \\[6pt] \sin\theta & \tfrac{\cos\theta}{r} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \end{pmatrix}. \]

Step 2. Second Derivatives — Full Expansion

Now compute

\[ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x^{2}} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\Big(\cos\theta \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} - \tfrac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\Big), \]
\[ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y^{2}} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\Big(\sin\theta \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} + \tfrac{\cos\theta}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\Big). \]

Recall from Step 1:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=\cos\theta \,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}-\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=\sin\theta \,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}+\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}. \]

We will also use the relations

\[ \frac{\partial r}{\partial x}=\cos\theta,\quad \frac{\partial r}{\partial y}=\sin\theta,\quad \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}=-\frac{\sin\theta}{r},\quad \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y}=\frac{\cos\theta}{r}, \]

and

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac1r\right)=-\frac{\cos\theta}{r^2},\qquad \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac1r\right)=-\frac{\sin\theta}{r^2}. \]

Compute \(\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x^{2}}\).

Write

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=A-B,\qquad A=\cos\theta\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r},\quad B=\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}. \]

Term \(A_x\):

\[ \frac{\partial A}{\partial x}=(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\cos\theta)\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}+\cos\theta\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\right). \]

Now

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\cos\theta=-\sin\theta\,\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}=\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r}, \]

and

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\right) = \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial r}\,\frac{\partial r}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}\,\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = \cos\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}} - \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial r \partial\theta}. \]

Thus

\[ \frac{\partial A}{\partial x}=\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}+\cos^2\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}} -\frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial r \partial\theta}. \]

Term \(B_x\):

\[ \frac{\partial B}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\right)\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\right). \]

Compute

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\right) =-\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2}, \]

and

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\right) =\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}\,\frac{\partial r}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta\,\partial \theta}\,\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} =\cos\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}-\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}. \]

Hence

\[ \frac{\partial B}{\partial x}=-\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta} -\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}. \]

Combine:

\[ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x^{2}} =\cos^2\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}} +\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} -\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta} +\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}. \]

Compute \(\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y^{2}}\).

Similarly,

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=C+D,\qquad C=\sin\theta\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r},\quad D=\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}. \]

Term \(C_y\):

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial y}=(\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\sin\theta)\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}+\sin\theta\,\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\right). \]

Now

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\sin\theta=\cos\theta\,\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y}=\frac{\cos^2\theta}{r}, \]

and

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\right) =\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial r}\,\frac{\partial r}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}\,\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} =\sin\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}}+\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}. \]

Thus

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial y}=\frac{\cos^2\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} +\sin^2\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}} +\frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}. \]

Term \(D_y\):

\[ \frac{\partial D}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\right)\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\right). \]

Now

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\right)=-\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2}, \]

and

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\right) =\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}\,\frac{\partial r}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta\,\partial \theta}\,\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} =\sin\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta}+\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}. \]

Thus

\[ \frac{\partial D}{\partial y}=-\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta} +\frac{\cos^2\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}. \]

Combine:

\[ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y^{2}} =\sin^2\theta\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}} +\frac{\cos^2\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} +\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r\,\partial \theta} -\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\cos^2\theta}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}. \]

Final Combination.

Add the two results:

\[ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y^{2}} =\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}}+\frac{1}{r}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}. \]
\[ \boxed{\,\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y^{2}} = \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial r^{2}}+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}}\,} \]

Final Result

Thus, the Laplace equation in polar coordinates is

\[ \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\Big(r \frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\Big) + \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial \theta^{2}} = 0. \]
Remark

This change of variables illustrates how PDEs can be transformed into more convenient coordinate systems. In particular, Laplace’s equation in polar form is crucial in problems with radial or angular symmetry.

Leibniz’s Rule

Consider a function defined by an integral:

\[ f(x) = \frac{d F(x)}{d x}, \qquad F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt. \]

Then by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus,

\[ \frac{d}{d x} \int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x). \]

If instead we write

\[ \int_x^a f(t)\,dt = F(a) - F(x), \]

then

\[ \frac{d}{d x} \int_x^a f(t)\,dt = -f(x). \]

General Leibniz Rule

If

\[ I(x) = \int_{u(x)}^{v(x)} f(x,t)\,dt, \]

then differentiating with respect to \(x\) gives

\[ \frac{d I}{d x} = f(x,v(x))\frac{d v}{d x} - f(x,u(x))\frac{d u}{d x} + \int_{u(x)}^{v(x)} \frac{\partial f(x,t)}{\partial x}\,dt. \]

This is known as Leibniz’s Rule.

Example - Leibniz rule with variable limits

Evaluate

\[ I(x) = \int_{x}^{2x} \frac{e^{xt}}{t}\,dt. \]
Solution

Step 1. Apply Leibniz’s rule

\[ \frac{d I}{d x} = \frac{e^{x(2x)}}{2x}\frac{d (2x)}{d x} - \frac{e^{x(x)}}{x}\frac{d (x)}{d x} + \int_{x}^{2x} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\Big(\frac{e^{xt}}{t}\Big)\,dt. \]

Step 2. Simplify

\[ \frac{d I}{d x} = \frac{e^{2x^2}}{2x}(2) - \frac{e^{x^2}}{x}(1) + \int_x^{2x} e^{xt}\,dt. \]
\[ \frac{d I}{d x} = \frac{e^{2x^2}}{x} - \frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + \Bigg[\frac{1}{x}e^{xt}\Bigg]_{t=x}^{2x}. \]

Step 3. Evaluate the integral

\[ \int_x^{2x} e^{xt}\,dt = \frac{1}{x}\Big(e^{2x^2} - e^{x^2}\Big). \]

Step 4. Collect terms

\[ \frac{d I}{d x} = \frac{e^{2x^2}}{x} - \frac{e^{x^2}}{x} + \frac{1}{x}\Big(e^{2x^2} - e^{x^2}\Big). \]
\[ \frac{d I}{d x} = \frac{2}{x}\Big(e^{2x^2} - e^{x^2}\Big). \]
Remark

Leibniz’s rule generalizes the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus by handling integrals with variable limits and integrands depending on the parameter \(x\).
